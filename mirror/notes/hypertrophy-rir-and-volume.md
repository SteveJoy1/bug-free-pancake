# Hypertrophy — effort (RIR) vs weekly volume

## Summary

My working model of hypertrophy: **weekly hard-set volume is the main lever; frequency matters mostly as a way to distribute that volume**. Going to true 0 RIR is not clearly superior to 1–2 RIR for growth, but if I've been stagnant despite consistent sessions, the most likely personal bug is that my "1–2 RIR" sets have actually been farther from failure than I think — so deliberately training closer to failure is a useful *calibration tool*, not a biological magic bullet.

## Evidence

- I framed the theory that "one quality + two mediocre workouts per week can produce hypertrophy" and asked for evaluation against evidence — the way I phrased "mediocre" (not "token") matched what the literature would endorse as compatible with growth.
- I raised a specific self-directed hypothesis: midrange ALMI for years with consistent gym sessions → I may have been providing too little stimulus, so pushing zero RIR despite the usual "1–2 RIR is fine" finding may help *me*. I correctly framed this as a personal Bayesian-update question, not a claim that 0 RIR is universally better.
- I asked the follow-up comparative question: suboptimal effort + high volume vs appropriate effort + suboptimal volume. I recognized that there isn't a clean head-to-head literature and that I needed synthesis.
- I pushed back on a redundant summary paragraph in the assistant's response — useful signal that I track when reasoning actually advances vs restates.

## Gaps to close

1. **RIR calibration.** Self-estimated RIR is noisy; using zero-RIR sets periodically is a cheap way to sanity-check whether my usual sets are actually close to failure.
2. **Volume floor.** The practical "good zone" commonly cited is ~12–20 hard sets per muscle per week; know whether I'm actually hitting that.
3. **Progression signal.** Adding load OR adding reps both drive hypertrophy — need to verify I'm actually progressing one of them, not just repeating.
4. **Low-load caveat.** Failure appears to matter *more* with low loads than with heavy loads (high-threshold motor units recruited only late in the set). If I drift toward lighter weights + farther-from-failure, the combination may be especially weak.
