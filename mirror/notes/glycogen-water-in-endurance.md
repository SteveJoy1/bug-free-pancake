# Glycogen-associated water and endurance hydration

## Summary

My intuition — "if glycogen is exported to fuel exercise, shouldn't its associated water also become available for sweat?" — is **directionally sensible but overstates how useful that water is**. Glycogen-associated water is mostly **ordinary intracellular water already part of normal osmotic equilibrium**, not a protected reserve that cashes in during dehydration. The paper I read modeled only a ~55 mL plasma-volume advantage from starting glycogen-replete over a 2 L sweat loss. So glycogen water is real but small as a hydration asset.

## Evidence

- I read the glycogen-water paper and tried to construct the **strongest opposite argument** — the liver-glycogen-exports-water thought experiment — rather than just accepting the conclusion. That's the right way to stress-test a claim.
- I used a **fasting-weight-loss analogy** to probe the framing: "if initial fasting weight loss is partly glycogen/water, that water *was* released and that's not dehydration — how does that square with the paper?" The resolution (fasting has a hormonal/renal natriuresis component that acute exercise doesn't) depends on timescale separation I had not explicitly considered.
- I asked whether the paper had **empirical before/after glycogen-depletion data** or was conceptual/modeling work — answer: mostly modeling, assumes osmotic stability before exercise.
- I correctly summarized the paper's conclusion ("excess water mostly stays in the cells") and then asked the right follow-up: **"if not glycogen keeping the water there, what is?"** — which is the real mechanistic question.
- The conversation closed with my synthesis — "other things shift over slower/different time horizons" — correctly linking the paper's acute-compartment model to the multi-day fasting observation.

## Gaps to close

1. **Osmotic biology.** Intracellular fluid is characterized by high K⁺, phosphates, proteins, and organic osmolytes; Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase actively maintains cell volume. Water follows total osmotic gradient, not glycogen specifically.
2. **Muscle vs liver glycogen differ.** Skeletal muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase so muscle glycogen is consumed *locally* — water redistribution is mostly intracellular/interstitial in-situ, not exported as plasma.
3. **Water intake ceiling during exercise.** ~0.4–1.0 L/h is the practical range, sweat rates can materially exceed this. 17 oz over 85 min (~0.35 L/h) is below common endurance-intake guidance.
4. **Half-marathon fueling is real but variable.** For a ~90 min effort, 30–60 g CHO/h is the guideline; "the wall at mile 9" is plausibly carbohydrate-limited but could also be pacing or heat.
5. **Fasting water loss ≠ pathological dehydration.** Regulated loss of stored water/sodium is not the same as uncompensated plasma-volume contraction with impaired function.
